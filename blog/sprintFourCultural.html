Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset...

How does understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefit people?

How does understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefit people? Neuroplasticity is the concept that the brain is malleable, it can adopt new ideas, new habits, and learn new things over time. Neuro refers to the neurons which are the nerves in the brain and these have the ability to make new connections and pathways. Plasticity refers to the idea of being malleable.

People who understand that the brain can make new connections, that it can build new pathways, are people who will be open to the idea that they can learn new tasks, new ideas, and achieve their goals.

Rather than being stuck in a mindeset that they are too old to learn, or that they are not clever enough to learn something, those people who accept neuroplasticity and the research findings will be open to learning and improving themselves.

How can neuroplasticity benefit me?

How can neuroplasticity benefit me? How can neuroplasticity benefit me? Neuroplasticity can benefit anyone and everyone. It's especially worthwhile understanding this if you're taking on new and exciting challenges.

Positive learning environments, challenges, repetition, as well as rest and exercise are all key factors when seeking the benefits of neuroplasticity. Revision is important, and using the new ideas that you've learned will help to form new connections in the brain.

Growth Mindset

Growth Mindset A person who has a fixed mindest believes that they cannot change, they cannot learn new things and they think that they are stuck with the abilities they have been given. In contrast to this, someone with a growth mindset is open to the malleableness of the brain, and the concept that they can form new connections in the brain, new pathways, and in doing so learn new, exciting, and challenging concepts both mental and physical.

When leaning new things it is important to come to the challenge with a growth mindset. The more you practise a task, and build on the task, the better you will become at it, and you will be able to add more and more learning on top of this.

I have found this to be true since I started learning HTML and CSS a year ago, slowly adding in more and more, building on the foundations I created. I think this is a key concept to becoming a web developer or software engineer - your success depends on having a growth mindset, on practising your skills everyday, trying new things, and building on the foundations you have, no matter how basic they may be.
