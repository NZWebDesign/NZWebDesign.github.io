Core Reflection...

Values, empathy and self awareness in learning and programming...

You've got to be able to get along with other people when you're in a learning or programming environment. People have different values, and regardless of your values and their values, you still need to be able to get along with them and get the job done. Empathy is important, as you can't just charge in and expect the other people in your team to be on the same page as you. They may have things going on in their lives which mean they are not in the best zone, and you've got to be able to recognise that and work with it. This leads on to self awareness, being aware to the fact that perhaps you went charging on into the work zone without consideration that your team mates dog just passed away, they're not in the work zone obviously, yet you displayed no self awareness to the fact that you just went straight into work mode and expected them to do the same.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

I don't recall there being anything terribly surprising in the core learning... I'm sure if it was surprising I'd recall it, and would be my 'go to' surprise to write about...

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

A lot of the core learning was interesting, the challenging aspects would be trying to feel motivated to do this sort of thing for the millionth time. The most challenging would be around identity and values exploration, for two reasons. 1) being the "here we go again" doing this sort of thing seems to be commonplace everytime you embark on some new tertiary or learning course. and 2) I'm quite happy with where I am in life, I know what I value, what my identity is, and the 'here we go again' comes into play, as I'm not going to be changing my identity or values.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development bootcamp course?

There'd be plenty of people out there who are quite happy beavering away on the laptop in their dark bedroom turning out code that NASA would be proud of, yet those same people often lack the skills to get their code to NASA. They often lack human interaction skills, the ability to communicate, the ability to work in a team. These days, it's all about the customer, you need to be able to work with the customer, communicate with the customer, and hear the customer. From an employers point of view, I'd rather employ someone with very basic coding ability, but who is personable, who is friendly and professional, and who is open to learning, and can demonstrate that they are teachable, the person that knows that they don't know everything...

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practising programming instead? Justify your answer.

Some of it was repetitious in that I've done plenty of this sort of thing already. However, I wouldn't say it was a waste of time, as it didn't use up much time. There were definitely aspects that were worth doing, the refreshers on Neuroplasticity and how people learn, and this is something I've been interested in for years anyway. It draws me back to high school when we had Lance King do workshops with us, and I still recall and use many takeaways from his workshops.
